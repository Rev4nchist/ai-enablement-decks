1
The Platform Vision — What He Actually Sees
His mental model of the end state. What he's building toward, not just what's live now.
Vision
When you imagine a customer's AI agent connecting to Fourth two years from now — walk me through what that looks like end to end. What does the customer's side look like, and what does Fourth's side do?
The most open question on the list. Let him paint the picture without steering. What you need from this: the physical experience of the connection — what does the customer actually do, what does Fourth actually return, what does "governed access" feel like from the customer's seat.
Vision
You mentioned getting me "inside the performance intelligence world" — what's the thing that, once I understand it, changes how I see everything else?
He said this directly in the June 4 meeting. It's an invitation he gave you — take it. What you need: the core concept that makes the whole platform make sense. Probably something about the intelligence layer being the product, not the application.
From his June 4 words: "Christian needs to get David inside the performance intelligence world to fully understand the vision." This is a door he already opened.
Structure
The ~300 tools — how are they organised? Is it one big catalogue, or grouped by system, by role, by use case?
What you need: the taxonomy of the tool catalogue. This is the foundation of the Developer Experience Portal — you can't design the portal until you know how the tools are grouped and discovered.
Structure
What's the difference between a tool and a skill in your model? Are they the same thing, or does the platform offer both?
He mentioned "skills packaged inside connectors" in the June 4 meeting. There's a distinction in his mind between raw data access (tools) and pre-packaged intelligence (skills). What you need: clarity on whether the platform serves raw operations or pre-built intelligence packages — or both, layered.
From his June 4 words: "Custom connectors allow packaging skills directly inside the connector itself. This eliminates the need for users to manage separate connectors and skills."
Listen for these signals
Whether he distinguishes between "accessing data" and "accessing intelligence" — these are different products
Any mention of the playbook — what it contains, who builds it, how it's maintained per location
Whether the 300 tools are already designed or still being conceived
Whether he uses "API" vs "agent" language — signals how he expects customers to connect
2
The Customer Connection — How It Actually Works
The mechanics of how a customer's AI tool connects. The flow nobody has documented yet.
Customer
If I'm a customer CIO and I want to connect my company's AI tool to Fourth — what's the actual first step? Do I get credentials, install something, configure something?
The onboarding flow is completely undocumented. What you need: the literal sequence — what does a customer IT team do from day one. This becomes the "Getting Started" section of the portal.
Structure
When a customer's AI tool sends a request — who authenticates it? Does the customer's user log in, or does the customer's IT team set up a service account, or something else?
The internal governance proxy uses per-user Microsoft identity. It's unclear if that model extends to external customers. What you need: the external auth model — is it their own Microsoft tenant, a Fourth-issued credential, or something else entirely.
Structure
You mentioned middleware that transforms vague prompts into specific actions. Where does that live — in Fourth's platform, or does the customer configure it, or is it something else?
What you need: whether the intelligence transformation (raw prompt → governed action) happens inside Fourth's layer or requires customer configuration. This changes what the customer experience looks like significantly.
From his June 4 words: "Middleware can transform vague prompts into actionable requests using Salesforce navigation knowledge. Multiple middleware layers can transform requests in different ways for higher quality outputs."
Customer
What does the customer actually get back? Is it data, a formatted answer, a dashboard, something they can act on — or do they decide?
The governed AI access deck shows "apps, live dashboards, generative UI" as outputs. What you need: whether the output format is fixed by Fourth, configurable by the customer, or driven entirely by the AI tool the customer brings. The answer shapes what CMA's role is.
Customer
Is there a customer who's already using this, or ready to pilot? If so, who — and what are they trying to do?
The most grounding question on the list. What you need: a real use case with a real customer, even if it's early or informal. If he names one, that becomes the reference implementation for everything.
Listen for these signals
Any mention of a specific customer, prospect, or pilot — even a "we're in conversations with..." signals what's real vs. theoretical
Whether he distinguishes between what a large enterprise customer vs. a restaurant consultant would do differently
How he handles the question of customers who use competitors' scheduling tools — can they still connect to Fourth's intelligence layer?
Any mention of pricing at the connection level — per call, per seat, subscription, or consumption-based
3
The Intelligence Layer — What Makes It Worth Connecting To
The "25 years of data" claim only has value if it's structured and accessible. What is it actually?
Vision
You mentioned that AI agents need the "playbook and context for each location" to actually be useful. What is the playbook — is it structured data, a document, something Fourth builds, or something the customer builds?
This is the core of the intelligence layer claim. What you need: is the playbook a Fourth-maintained knowledge base about how hospitality operations work, or is it customer-specific configuration, or both — and who is responsible for keeping it current.
From his June 4 words: "Simply feeding all restaurant data to an AI asking 'what should I schedule next week?' produces poor results without location-specific context. The intelligence platform provides this crucial context layer."
Structure
How does Fourth's 25 years of data get turned into something a customer's AI agent can actually use? Is it pre-processed into benchmarks, surfaced on demand, or does it work differently?
What you need: whether the intelligence layer is proactively packaged (benchmarks, patterns, norms) or reactively assembled (the AI asks, Fourth queries). This determines the architecture significantly.
Structure
When a customer's agent asks about a specific location — how does Fourth know what's specific to that location vs. what's general hospitality knowledge?
What you need: the data model for location-specificity. Is this customer-configured, or does Fourth derive it from operational data, or both. Critical for understanding what the portal needs to help customers set up.
Limits
Is the intelligence layer limited to customers who are on Fourth's platform, or can a customer using a competitor's scheduling tool still get value from connecting to Fourth?
He mentioned this directly — "Fourth will be able to ingest data from competitors' systems." What you need: the concrete model for how that works. Does the customer import competitor data to Fourth, or does Fourth reach out to it, or something else.
From his June 4 words: "Fourth will be able to ingest data from competitors' systems and still provide the intelligence layer. The platform becomes a cost center/revenue stream even when customers use competitors' tools."
Listen for these signals
Whether he differentiates between "operational data" (what happened) and "intelligence" (what it means) — the gap between them is the product
Any mention of benchmarking — comparing a location to industry norms or similar sites is likely the primary intelligence value-add
Whether customers configure the intelligence layer or Fourth does it on their behalf
Any mention of data quality or freshness — how current does the context need to be to be useful
4
Governance & Trust — What the Customer's IT Team Cares About
The trust center, permissions, compliance. What Christian has already built and what's still needed.
Structure
You mentioned building a trust center. What does a customer actually see when they open it? What can they do there?
He mentioned the trust center multiple times in the June 4 meeting but never described its interface. What you need: is it a dashboard, a reporting feed, an issue log, an admin panel — and who at the customer accesses it (CIO, IT team, end users).
From his June 4 words: "Christian has built a trust center where customers can report AI issues, data concerns, and problems. Issues route to both the customer's central trust center and Fourth's trust center for monitoring and correction."
Structure
When a customer's CIO asks "what has your AI done with our data?" — what can Fourth show them today, and what are you building to show them in six months?
What you need: the concrete answer to the data governance question every enterprise CIO will ask. This is both a technical specification and a sales message.
Customer
How does a customer control what their AI agents are allowed to do in Fourth? Can a CIO say "this agent can read but not write" or "this agent can only access location X"?
What you need: the permission model from the customer's perspective. The internal model uses persona routing — does the external model expose the same concept, and is it self-serve or Fourth-configured.
Limits
What are the non-negotiables from a governance perspective — things you'll never allow any external AI agent to do regardless of what the customer wants?
What you need: the hard limits. This defines the boundary of the platform and tells you what the portal documentation needs to be explicit about up front.
Listen for these signals
Whether the trust center is already built and live, or conceptual — the June 4 notes say "Christian has built" it, but the scope is unclear
Whether the write/action capability is ever planned for external customers, or deliberately kept read-only
Any mention of compliance requirements (SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA for healthcare hospitality) that shape what's permitted
Whether customers see each other's data in any way, or if isolation is total

The Developer Experience Portal — What He Built and What He Wants
He built a 4-hour prototype site. Dave owns this going forward. What's the brief?
Vision
When you imagine a customer's developer team landing on the portal for the first time — what's the one thing you want them to understand in the first 30 seconds?
He has strong opinions about this (not a 57-page docs site, keep it visual and live). What you need: the single message the portal must land. This becomes the hero of every page Dave builds.
Structure
You built the initial site in four hours. Can you walk me through it — what sections are there, what were you trying to show with each one?
The site is at rev4nchist.github.io/ai-enablement-decks/governed-ai-access/ — Dave has seen it. But asking Christian to walk through his own intent will surface what he was trying to communicate, not just what's there. What you need: his mental model of the portal structure, in his words.
Already captured from the site: The deck covers: the core idea, why a governance proxy, the 4-step connection flow, governance features live today, the connector foundation, the multiplier (cross-system), beyond-text outputs, and current state/roadmap.
Customer
Who's the primary person you imagine using the portal — a developer writing integration code, a CIO evaluating whether to connect, or someone else?
He mentioned developers, engineers, CIOs, IT departments, restaurant consultants. These have very different needs. What you need: who he'd design the portal for if he could only pick one audience, and whether the others get their own paths or a shared experience.
Structure
What does "working example" look like to you — code someone can copy, a live demo they can interact with, a video, or something else?
He explicitly wants examples, not documentation. What you need: the format he has in mind so Dave builds the right things — runnable code samples, interactive sandboxes, recorded demos, or live environments.
Timing
What's the minimum state the portal needs to reach before you'd show it to a real customer or prospect?
What you need: his bar for what "ready to show externally" means. This is the MVP definition Dave needs to work from.
Listen for these signals
Any mention of a specific feature or section he's tried to build but wasn't happy with — those are the pain points to solve first
Whether he sees the portal as marketing (discovery and evangelism) or technical documentation (integration support) — they require different structures
Whether webinars and live content are part of his model, or whether he sees the site itself as the primary channel
Any frustration with how the current site doesn't communicate what he wants — this is the brief in disguise

Go-to-Market & Timing — What Has to Be True Before This Launches
The sequence, the gates, the first customer. He's deliberate about not rushing this.
Timing
What's the thing that has to be true before you'd let an external customer connect their AI to Fourth — technically, commercially, or from a trust perspective?
What you need: his personal launch gate. This is more important than a timeline — it tells you what to build toward.
Customer
Who's the first customer you'd want to try this — do you have someone in mind, or is it a type of customer rather than a specific name?
What you need: a real name or profile. If it's a specific customer, everything should be designed against that pilot. If it's a type, that type needs to be defined precisely enough to build for.
Timing
How do you see this being sold or introduced — does it go through the existing sales team, is it self-serve, or is it a different motion entirely?
What you need: whether this is a top-down CTO/CIO sale, a self-serve developer adoption, or a product-led motion. This determines who the portal is actually designed to convince.
Limits
What's the thing you're most worried about getting wrong on the external customer side — technically, commercially, or reputationally?
He said CIOs who "get it in seconds" are the ones who understand head office transformation. The others will "be hit like a truck" by AI sprawl. What you need: what keeps him up at night about this specifically. His top risk is the best signal for what to design defensively.
Vision
You described this as creating a new category — a network platform. What does Fourth need to have done in 18 months for that claim to be credible?
What you need: success looks like what, specifically. Not KPIs — the concrete evidence that would tell him (and tell Carly and Scott) that the bet paid off.
From his June 4 words: "Success depends on whether Fourth is 'truly creating a new category' and getting others to adopt the platform." He knows this is the measure — ask him to make it concrete.
Listen for these signals
Whether he has a specific customer, pilot, or partner in mind — any proper noun here is gold
The "grind element" he mentioned — ecosystem building takes time. Does he have a timeframe in mind, or is he deliberately not committing to one?
Any mention of Scott (CEO) or board-level visibility on this — signals how strategically prioritised this actually is
Whether pricing is designed or still being worked out — if he hasn't landed on a model yet, that's a decision Dave can help shape
