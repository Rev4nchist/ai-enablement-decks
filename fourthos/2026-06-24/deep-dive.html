Flagship Deep Dive — Connector Ecosystem

Active / Yellow — the live foundation is strong; gated reporting and the IT-grant path are the near-term bottleneck.

The thesis

The connector is the mold.

The Salesforce connector isn't a one-off integration — it's the mold. Build the governance once (identity, audit, read-only access, reporting, feedback) and every future connector is stamped from it. Governed, composable connectors are the data-and-action substrate for FourthOS performance intelligence, not just another integration pile.

Why this matters

The hard part — per-user identity, default-deny, audit, governed reporting — is solved once and inherited. Connector #2 writes connector logic, not governance. That is reusable leverage, not a one-off win.

Expected outcome

A reusable connector pattern that starts live with Salesforce, proves itself with Zendesk, then scales to NetSuite and Jira — each inheriting identity, audit, reporting, and feedback automatically.

Architecture

Build the mold once — every stamp inherits the governance

Every request flows through one governed checkpoint. The live proxy resolves per-user identity, enforces read-only / default-deny access, and writes an append-only audit trail — then brokers the call to the live system. Its reporting engine and feedback loop are built in-repo. Future connectors are stamped from the same mold and inherit all four assets.

Concept spotlight

Here's the idea
Governance proxy (FastMCP v3)

A connector that sits between an AI agent and a business system like Salesforce. It enforces who you are (your own identity, via Entra then on-behalf-of token exchange), what you can see (read-only, default-deny), and records what happened (full audit) — so AI acts under each person's own permissions, not one shared god-key. Build the mold once and every future connector (Zendesk, NetSuite, Jira) inherits the same identity, audit, reporting, and feedback standard.

Reporting spotlight

One week of audit data, three governed files Built In-Repo

Plain-English default-deny: a report tier can only ever contain what its audience is entitled to see. The same week of connector audit data renders into Leadership, IT, and Governance files — and the Leadership file you'd email out physically does not contain the per-user rows. The payoff

What the ecosystem unlocks (impossible with point integrations)

A regional manager asks Claude: "Which of my Tier-1 accounts are at risk right now — and draft the save-play?"

One governed turn. One identity. Four legs — each scoped to this manager's own permissions:

Salesforce Live · Alpha At-risk accounts in this manager's territory + renewal dates — only the fields this manager may see (per-user FLS).
Zendesk Next Each account's open P1 tickets + support-load trend (org-scoped).
NetSuite Future AR aging >60 days under this user's finance role — not a god-mode account.
Fourth labor telemetry Future Is the at-risk site also understaffed vs forecast? The signal no external vendor has.

Joined by an explicit, confidence-scored entity match that degrades honestly — "matched 0.7 — confirm?" — never a silent wrong join.

One Leadership-tier brief through the default-deny serializer — a field this manager isn't entitled to cannot appear.
One signed receipt — this human, this time, read these records across four systems, and mutated nothing.

A point-integration competitor gets three disconnected dumps, three identities, no join key.
