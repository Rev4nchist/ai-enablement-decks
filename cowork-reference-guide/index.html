The Cowork Reference Guide

Everything you need to understand Cowork, build your workspace, and use Claude like it actually knows your job. Written for people who've used ChatGPT or Claude chat — no prior setup experience required.

Fourth AI Enablement March 2026 ~20 min read

01 What is Cowork?

You've probably used ChatGPT or Claude before — typed a question, got an answer, started a new conversation, and lost all context. Every session starts from zero. Claude doesn't know your name, your role, your company, or what you worked on yesterday.

Cowork changes that.

Cowork is a mode of Claude that gives it a persistent workspace — a folder on your computer with files that tell Claude who you are, how you work, what you're working on, and what tools you use. Instead of starting every conversation blank, Claude reads your workspace at the start of each session and picks up where you left off.

Think of it this way:

Regular ClaudeCowork
New intern every morning — needs everything explainedSharp colleague who was briefed before you walked in
Forgets everything between conversationsRemembers your projects, team, preferences, and terminology
Generic responses to generic promptsSpecific, contextual responses calibrated to your role
You adapt to the toolThe tool adapts to you
The real difference

Cowork doesn't make Claude smarter. It makes Claude informed. The same way a new hire becomes useful not when they learn more, but when they understand your team, your priorities, and your way of working.

02 Before & After

Here's what the same request looks like with and without a properly configured workspace:

Without Cowork

You: "Summarise this meeting transcript"

Claude: Generic bullet list of topics discussed. No context on who matters, what's urgent, or what action to take. You spend 10 minutes editing it into something useful.

With Cowork

You: "Summarise this meeting transcript"

Claude: Executive summary led by the decision. Your action items separated from others. Strategic flags about things you'd miss. Suggested next steps routed to the right person. Saved to your outputs folder. Done.

The difference isn't the prompt. It's the context. Claude knows your team, your priorities, your communication style, and your filing system — because you told it once, in your workspace files.

03 How Claude Thinks

Before building your workspace, it helps to understand how Claude processes information. This isn't technical — it's practical.

The Context Window

Claude has a "working memory" — everything it can see at once in a conversation. This includes your messages, its responses, any files you've shared, AND your workspace files. Think of it as a desk: the more relevant documents on the desk, the better the work.

System Prompts and Instructions

When you start a Cowork session, Claude reads your CLAUDE.md file first — before you say anything. This is like a briefing document that a colleague reads before a meeting. It tells Claude: "Here's who I am, here's what I'm working on, here's how I want you to communicate."

The quality of this briefing document directly determines the quality of Claude's help.

Skills and Plugins

Skills are pre-written workflows that Claude can follow. Instead of you explaining a process every time ("take this transcript, extract decisions, list action items with owners..."), a skill contains those instructions. You just trigger it: "Parse this meeting." Claude loads the skill, follows the steps, and produces a structured output.

Plugins bundle multiple skills, tool connections, and configurations into installable packages. More on both in the Skills and Plugins sections.

Tool Connections (MCPs)

By default, Claude can only see what you paste into the conversation. Tool connections let Claude reach into your actual work systems — Slack, Asana, Outlook, Notion — to read messages, search documents, create tasks, and more. This is where Cowork goes from "helpful chat" to "operational assistant."

Key insight

Everything in Cowork is about giving Claude the right context. Workspace files give it knowledge about you. Skills give it knowledge about processes. Tool connections give it access to your live data. All three together is where the real value is.

04 Workspace Anatomy

A Cowork workspace is a folder on your computer. Claude reads it at the start of every session. Here's the structure:

my-workspace/ ├── CLAUDE.md ← The brain. Claude reads this first. Your operating instructions. ├── TASKS.md ← Running task list. Updated across sessions. ├── context/ ← Who you are, how you work, your company, your team. │ ├── who-i-am.md │ ├── how-i-talk.md │ ├── how-you-work.md │ ├── company.md │ └── team-directory.md ├── memory/ ← Persistent knowledge. Glossary, people profiles, project notes. │ └── glossary.md └── outputs/ ← Everything Claude generates. Organised by type. └── CLAUDE.md ← Filing rules for outputs.

Every file has a job. None are optional if you want a workspace that works well. Let's walk through each one.

05 CLAUDE.md — The Brain

This is the most important file in your workspace. Claude reads it at the start of every session. It's your standing instructions — the briefing document that shapes every response Claude gives you.

What goes in CLAUDE.md

  • Session-start instructions: Which context files to load and in what order
  • Workspace map: What's in each folder and what it's for
  • Current priorities: What you're working on right now (update this regularly)
  • Preferences: Communication style, output formats, theme choices, VP vocabulary
  • Quick-reference terms: The 10 most relevant terms for your role

What makes a good CLAUDE.md vs. a bad one

Weak
I work in marketing at Fourth.
Help me with content and emails.
Be concise.
Strong
## Current Priorities
1. Q2 campaign launch — email sequences due April 4
2. Blog pipeline — 3 posts/week, brand voice review
3. Competitive research for SHRM conference pitch
4. Weekly content performance report for VP Marketing

## Preferences
| Topic | Preference |
|-------|-----------|
| Style | Direct, scannable, data-backed |
| Brand | Fourth iQ (lowercase i, uppercase Q) |
| Tone | Energetic, insightful, human |
| Format | Bullets for lists, two-phase delivery |

The strong version tells Claude exactly what you're working on, how you want to communicate, and what brand rules to follow. Every session starts with this context already loaded.

Common mistake

People write their CLAUDE.md once and never update it. Your priorities change weekly. If CLAUDE.md says your priority is a project that finished last month, Claude will keep optimising for it. Update your priorities every Monday. It takes 2 minutes and changes everything.

06 Context Files

The context/ folder holds the stable facts about you and your environment. These change less often than CLAUDE.md — think of them as your professional profile that Claude references when it needs background.

who-i-am.md

Your identity, role, reporting structure, and what you spend time on. Claude uses this to understand the weight of your requests — a VP asking for a report gets different framing than an IC asking for a draft.

how-i-talk.md

Your communication preferences. Do you want bullets or prose? Direct or detailed? This also includes Fourth's brand voice rules (energetic, insightful, human, confident) and the VP Translation table — the standard conversions from technical language to executive language.

how-you-work.md

Operating rules: what Claude should just do (research, draft, organise files) vs. what it should ask permission for (delete files, send to external tools). Also covers your quality bar — what "done" looks like.

company.md

Fourth company context. This is the same for everyone: products, proof points, key teams, NorthStar program, brand essentials. You don't need to write this — it's provided.

team-directory.md

Your direct team, key stakeholders, and what they care about. When you say "send this to Carly," Claude knows Carly is the VP of Transformation who cares about measurable outcomes and data-driven updates.

07 Memory & Glossary

The memory/ folder is Claude's long-term knowledge base. The most important file here is glossary.md.

glossary.md

Contains three things:

  1. Fourth brand terms — iQ capitalisation, product names, taglines
  2. Company acronyms — ELT, SLT, CSM, EBR, SPARK, ARR, NRR, and dozens more
  3. VP-Friendly Translations — the conversion table from technical terms to executive language

When Claude sees "commits" in your meeting notes and needs to write a report for Carly, it translates to "capabilities delivered" automatically — because the glossary told it to.

You can add department-specific terms here too. If your team says "tiger team" or "war room" or uses shorthand that an outsider wouldn't understand, put it in the glossary. Claude will decode it without asking.

08 Outputs Folder

Everything Claude generates — reports, presentations, guides, dashboards — goes in outputs/. This folder has its own CLAUDE.md with filing rules:

FolderWhat Goes HereNaming
guides/How-to guides, tutorials{topic}-guide.html
presentations/Slide decks, reports{YYYY-MM-DD}-{topic}.html
dashboards/Dashboards, data viz{name}-dashboard.html
reports/Analysis, one-pagers{YYYY-MM-DD}-{topic}-report.ext

The rules keep your outputs organised across sessions. Without them, Claude creates files with random names in random locations.

09 What Are Skills?

Skills are reusable workflows that Claude can follow. Instead of writing instructions every time, a skill packages them into a file that Claude loads when triggered.

Think of it like a recipe vs. cooking from memory. You could tell Claude step-by-step how to parse a meeting transcript every time. Or you could have a meeting-parser skill that knows the format, extracts the right sections, handles edge cases, and saves the output correctly — every time, consistently.

What a skill contains

  • A trigger description — what phrases activate it ("parse this meeting", "summarise this call")
  • A workflow — the exact steps Claude follows
  • An output format — what the result looks like
  • Optional reference files — supporting data, templates, or scripts the skill uses

10 Using Skills

Skills activate based on what you say. You don't need to type a special command — just describe what you need in natural language.

Not needed

"Please activate the meeting-parser skill and run it on this transcript"

Just say

"Parse this meeting" or "What were the action items from this call?"

Claude recognises the trigger phrases and loads the skill automatically. If a skill exists for what you're asking, it'll use it.

How to know a skill is active

When Claude loads a skill, you'll see a brief indicator that the skill has been activated. The output will follow the skill's structured format rather than a generic response.

Skills in your workspace

Your workspace can also have local skills in a skills/ folder. These are custom to your setup — workflows that are specific to your role or department. The Scaffold skill (covered next) can help identify which skills are most relevant for you.

11 The Scaffold Skill

This is how you build your workspace without doing it manually.

The Cowork Scaffold skill is a guided interview — about 10 questions — that generates your entire workspace: CLAUDE.md, context files, memory, glossary, outputs structure, and task tracker. All populated with real content based on your answers.

How it works

1

Start the interview

Open a Cowork session and say: "Set up my Cowork workspace" or "Help me build my workspace." The Scaffold skill activates and starts asking questions.

2

Answer the questions

Some are multiple choice (department, tools, output types). Some ask for your own words (weekly rhythm, team members, department jargon). Give detail. "I make reports" produces a generic workspace. "I produce weekly EBR presentations for enterprise customers using data from the macro workbook" produces a workspace that actually helps.

3

Review what's generated

The skill creates all files and shows you a summary: what was built, recommended tool connections, and your first three sessions to try.

4

Start using it

Open a new session in your workspace. Claude loads your context automatically. Ask it something you'd normally spend 30 minutes on — a meeting summary, a draft email, a report outline. Notice the difference.

Donna says

The interview takes 10-15 minutes. That's the upfront investment that determines whether Claude works like a sharp colleague or a confused chatbot for every session that follows. The people who invest the time are the ones who stop rewriting the same instructions every conversation.

12 Creating Custom Skills

The built-in skills cover common workflows, but the real power is building skills for your specific processes. The Skill Forge is the tool for that.

What Skill Forge does

Skill Forge creates, tests, and optimises skills through a structured workflow. You describe the process you want to automate — the inputs, the steps, the output format — and Skill Forge builds a production-ready skill file with trigger descriptions, reference data, and evaluation tests.

When to use it

  • You do the same multi-step process repeatedly — weekly reports, client updates, data formatting, review checklists
  • You want consistent output quality — same structure, same sections, same brand compliance every time
  • You want to share a workflow with your team — a skill built once works for everyone in the same workspace or plugin

How to use it

Say something like: "Create a skill for [your process]" or "Build a skill that [does what you need]." Skill Forge walks you through defining the workflow, generates the skill, then runs parallel evaluations comparing the new skill against a baseline to verify it actually improves output quality.

Best practice

Start by describing a process you've explained to Claude more than twice. If you've written the same instructions three times, that's a skill waiting to be built. Skill Forge turns your tribal knowledge into a reusable, testable workflow.

13 Plugins

Plugins are packaged bundles of skills, tool connections, and configurations that install as a single unit. If skills are recipes, plugins are complete cookbooks — curated sets of workflows designed for a specific domain or role.

What a plugin contains

  • Multiple related skills — a coordinated set of workflows that work together
  • MCP configurations — pre-configured tool connections the skills depend on
  • Reference data — templates, brand assets, evaluation criteria

Examples at Fourth

PluginWhat It BundlesBest For
Fourth EBR BuilderData prep + competitive research + presentation suite (3 skills)CSMs building executive business reviews
Fourth Sales BrainDeal coach + RFP responder + product specialist + orchestrator (4 skills)Sales reps preparing for meetings
Enterprise SearchMulti-source search + digest + knowledge synthesis (5 skills)Anyone searching across work tools

Plugins are installed in Cowork settings. Once installed, their skills activate automatically — same as workspace-level skills, but maintained centrally by the team that built them.

Skills vs. plugins

Workspace skills are local to your folder — custom workflows only you use. Plugins are shared, versioned, and can be installed by anyone. If you build a skill that your whole team should use, Skill Forge can package it into a plugin for distribution.

14 What Are Tool Connections?

By default, Claude can only see what you paste into the conversation. Tool connections (technically called MCPs — Model Context Protocol) let Claude reach directly into your work systems.

Connected Claude can:

  • Read your Slack channels — search messages, summarise discussions, find decisions
  • Search your email and calendar — find meeting details, prep for calls, review threads
  • Create and manage Asana tasks — turn action items into tracked work without leaving the conversation
  • Search SharePoint and Notion — find documents, policies, and knowledge base content
  • Read and write Confluence pages — update documentation directly

This is where Cowork goes from "helpful chat" to "operational tool." The workspace gives Claude context about you. Tool connections give Claude access to your live data.

17 Writing Good Instructions

The biggest mistake people make with Cowork isn't the technology — it's writing vague instructions. Claude follows what you tell it. If the instructions are thin, the output is thin.

Rules for good CLAUDE.md instructions

  • Be specific about format. "Use bullet points for multi-item responses, prose for explanations" is better than "be clear."
  • Name your stakeholders. "Reports go to Carly Hodges (VP Transformation) who cares about measurable outcomes" — now Claude knows how to frame every report.
  • State what NOT to do. "Never use passive voice in customer comms" or "Don't use corporate jargon — translate everything to plain language." Constraints shape output as much as instructions do.
  • Include examples of what "good" looks like. If you have a report you loved, a meeting summary that nailed the format — include a sample. Claude pattern-matches from examples better than from abstract rules.
  • Update your priorities regularly. Stale priorities produce stale recommendations.

18 Prompting That Works

With a properly set up workspace, you need less complex prompts — the context does the heavy lifting. But good prompting still matters.

Patterns that work well

PatternExampleWhy it works
Give the end state"I need a one-pager Carly can share with ELT"Claude knows the audience, format, and quality bar
Name the constraint"Keep this under 200 words"Prevents Claude from over-producing
Provide the raw material"Here's the transcript — parse it"Skills handle the rest
Ask for what you'd miss"What am I not seeing in this plan?"Uses Claude's strength at pattern-matching across contexts
Iterate, don't restart"Make the executive summary sharper"Claude improves in-session better than from scratch
Anti-pattern

Don't write a 200-word prompt explaining what you want when a 10-word prompt + a good workspace achieves the same thing. The workspace is the detailed instruction. The prompt is the trigger. If you're writing complex prompts every time, your workspace needs work.

21 Build Your Workspace

You have two options:

Option A: Use the Scaffold Skill (recommended)

Open a Cowork session and say:

"Set up my Cowork workspace"

The skill runs a 10-question interview and generates everything — CLAUDE.md, context files, memory, glossary, outputs structure, and task tracker. All populated with your actual information. Takes 10-15 minutes.

Option B: Build manually

Create the folder structure from the Workspace Anatomy section. Write each file yourself using the guidance in sections 5-8. Use the example CLAUDE.md formats as a starting point. This takes longer but gives you full control over every line.

Recommendation

Use Option A (the Scaffold skill) to generate the initial workspace, then customise it. You get a working setup in 15 minutes and can refine it as you go — instead of staring at blank files trying to figure out what to write.

22 Your First 3 Sessions

1

Test drive

Ask Claude something you'd normally spend 30 minutes on: a meeting summary, a draft email, a report outline, a competitive brief. Notice how the context changes the output quality.

2

Refine

After your first 2-3 sessions, you'll notice gaps — things Claude gets wrong, context it's missing, formats it doesn't nail. Edit CLAUDE.md directly. Add what's missing, remove what doesn't apply. This is normal and expected.

3

Connect

Set up your recommended tool connections. Start with Slack and M365. This is where Cowork goes from "better chat" to "operational tool." Once Claude can read your messages and search your documents, the use cases multiply.
